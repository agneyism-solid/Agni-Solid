I’m very impressed with this, Tim Berners-Lee’s project: Solid (Soial Linked Data). I believe I must have initially stumbled across it in the neighborhood of the World Wide Web Consortium (W3), which I visit often for help with HTML and CSS technicalities. Later, while giving thought to some deplatforming concerns I’ve had for a while, I remembered Solid, and went back to research what they’re doing and how they work.

Tim Berners-Lee is certainly a name worthy of cyber-respect, and I like also that he is a Unitarian Universalist. He’s been working on the “Web 3.0,” which is known as the “Semantic Web,” its predecessor the “Web 2.0” being known as the “Social Web,” where we all got into Facebook, and blogging, and wikis, and so forth. Well, I found Wikipedia’s article Solid (web decentralization project) and went to investigate via their website at Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT).

The page begins by explaining “What is Solid?”:
Solid is an exciting new project led by Prof. Tim Berners-Lee, inventor of the World Wide Web, taking place at MIT. The project aims to radically change the way Web applications work today, resulting in true data ownership as well as improved privacy.
I followed the links from there, with encouragements like:
The best resource for all things Solid is now the Solid Community Site, supported by Inrupt. There you will find regular community updates, a wealth of developer resources and documentation, and general information about how Solid works,[1]
and:
Solid is decentralized, so there is no central place to “signup.” Instead, you can register an identity and get a POD on a Solid server, anywhere in the world. You can even stand up your own![2]
until finally:
Welcome to the Solid Prototype
This is a prototype implementation of a Solid server. It is a fully functional server, but there are no security or stability guarantees. If you have not already done so, please create an account.[3]


That’s my basic introduction. I’m going to be building my website here, freer than I’ve been elsewhere yet, on the Internet, to write anything I choose (of course it has to be legal, but the ethics of my own religiosity and so forth are mine to decide, without too much worry about who happens to like it). Thanks, Tim Berners-Lee!

I have started some other projects on various sites, which I‘ll consolidate here or at least reference as I’m able. Thank you, very much, my audience. MIT’s mention of “improved privacy” is great, but really I just feel a lot of appreciation for the truer friends who have stayed with me through these years of experimentation and development.
